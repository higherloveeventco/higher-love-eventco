Why I Built a Wedding Planning Company Without Packages

By Kylie Founder of Higher Love Event Co.

For about four years, I tried to start this business and kept stopping myself.

I'd get excited, start taking the steps to make it real, and then hit the same wall every time: I couldn't figure out what I had to add. The Portland wedding industry already had plenty of coordinators. Plenty of beautiful tablescapes. Plenty of Instagram-perfect moments captured in golden hour light. If that was all I was bringing, I didn't want to bother.

So I'd go back to working for someone else.

I spent six years inside this industry from almost every angle. I started coordinating luxury weddings in 2019, the kind where the budgets were staggering and the venues were the most exclusive in Portland. I managed venue sales for two Portland spaces, helped give them brand identities, learned how the business actually worked from the inside. Then I moved into full-scale event production, corporate events, large cultural weddings, multi-day productions. I learned a lot. I also started to notice things.

The wedding industry is built on a very particular idea of what a wedding should look like. Big. Elaborate. Photogenic. Packaged. And I understood why. There's real craft and real work behind a well-planned wedding, and it deserves to be compensated fairly. But somewhere along the way, the focus had drifted. Away from the people in the room. Toward how the room would look in photos.

I watched couples, real people starting their lives together, spend enormous amounts of money trying to meet a standard that mostly exists online. The guest experience became secondary to the aesthetic. Food got thrown away, flowers got tossed, disposable everything piled up in service of a single perfect shot.

There has to be a more honest way to do this.

What finally unlocked Higher Love wasn't a business insight. It was more of a cultural one. Something shifted in the last few years. I could feel it in the conversations I was having with Portland couples. People were tired of the bloat. Budgets stretched past the point of comfort for a single day. Waste that nobody talked about. Guest experiences that got swallowed by production. What I kept hearing instead was: we want it to feel real. We want people to actually have a good time. We want to spend our money on things that matter. That shift is what Higher Love is built around.

That shift is bigger than weddings. People aren't doing things the same way anymore — in how they eat, how they work, how they spend, how they celebrate. They want options that fit their actual lives, not a predetermined version of what their lives are supposed to look like. And yet the wedding industry is still largely selling the same all-or-nothing packages it built twenty years ago, designed around a model of weddings that fewer and fewer couples actually want.

That's when I knew what Higher Love was. Not another coordinator selling pretty. A coordinator who believed the planning experience should reflect the couple it's built for — not the industry it came from.

How it works

Every couple starts with Essential Coordination, expert month-of management beginning six weeks before the wedding. That's your foundation. From there, you add planning support only when you actually need it, billed at a flat hourly rate. Vendor research. Budget building. Design direction. Guest management. You choose what serves you. You only pay for that.

We believe in transparent, published pricing. Because your time matters and so does your trust.

Rooted in St. Johns

Higher Love is located in St. Johns, Portland. When possible, we keep the celebration spending in this neighborhood — venues, caterers, florists, photographers, bar service. Vendors I know personally, have worked with, and trust. St. Johns has an extraordinary community of small, independent businesses. Weddings are a real opportunity to put money into them.

After every wedding I coordinate, leftover catering is donated to the St. Johns Village, a transitional shelter right here in the neighborhood. I handle the logistics myself. It costs the couple nothing. The florals are picked up the day after and delivered to hospice. That one is personal. Hospice workers cared for my mom in her final moments in 2015, and this is one small way I know to give something back.

On sustainability: real glassware and tableware over disposables, every time. It photographs better, it produces less waste, and rental costs are usually lower than people expect. When rentals aren't an option, we go compostable. It's a small thing that adds up across a hundred weddings.

Pricing is published. All of it. No inquiry required just to find out if we're in your budget. That one decision has shaped every conversation I have with prospective clients — and I think it's the right way to do business.

Portland is exactly the right place for a business like this. People here tend to ask questions, care about where their money goes, and have a healthy skepticism of industries that obscure their pricing. I fit right in.

That's what was worth building.
